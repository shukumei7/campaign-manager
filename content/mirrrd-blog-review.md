## Critical Issues

### 1. Duplicate Content — SEO Penalty Risk

Two near-identical Jacob Elordi posts and two near-identical Connor Storrie posts exist simultaneously. Google treats duplicate or near-duplicate content as low quality and will suppress both pages in search rankings.

**Action required:**
- Delete either post #4 or #7 (Jacob Elordi). Keep whichever is better written; differentiate significantly if both are kept.
- Delete either post #5 or #6 (Connor Storrie). Same approach.

### 2. Fake Awards Show Name — Credibility Killer

The Demi Moore post (#3) attributes her look to the **"2026 Actor Awards"** — this is not a real awards show name. No major ceremony uses this name. The likely real event is the **SAG Awards (Screen Actors Guild Awards)** or possibly the **Golden Globes**. Demi Moore won the Golden Globe for Best Actress (Musical or Comedy) for *The Substance* at the January 2025 Golden Globes.

**Action required:** Identify and use the correct awards show name. If the event is uncertain, remove the specific event reference and describe it as "a recent red carpet appearance."

### 3. Unverified Celebrity — Connor Storrie

Connor Storrie does not appear to be a widely recognisable public figure at the level warranted by two dedicated blog posts claiming he "dominated the 2026 red carpet." Posts about unknown or marginal figures will not rank for any meaningful search queries and will read as fabricated to visitors who try to look him up.

**Action required:** Verify Connor Storrie is a real, searchable public figure with genuine red carpet coverage. If not, replace both posts with a verified celebrity who was actually prominent at the 2026 Golden Globes.

## Quality Issues

### 4. Identical Template Across All Posts

Every post follows the exact same structure: **Hair → Grooming → Clothing → Fitness**. This is correct for the mirrrd.com product alignment (which also covers these four domains), but the execution is mechanical — posts read as AI-generated batch content rather than genuine style editorial.

The fitness section is particularly out of place in posts that are primarily about hair and clothing. A post titled "Margot Robbie's Premiere Look" does not need a push/pull/legs split.

**Action required:** Vary structure by post type. A red carpet look post should focus on the specific event outfit and hair. A "style evolution" post can be broader. Reserve the full 4-domain treatment for "how to look like [celebrity]" evergreen guides.

### 5. No Product CTAs

No post mentions or links to mirrrd.com. These posts exist to drive traffic that converts to free analyses and paid top-ups — that loop is currently broken. The posts attract visitors searching for celebrity style advice, but do nothing to route them into the product.

**Action required:** Add a contextual CTA to every post. See the blogging spec (`templates/blog_post.md`) for approved CTA copy.

### 6. No Sources or Event References

Posts make specific factual claims (what someone wore, at what event) with no links, image credits, or source references. Style editorial without photo references is unusual and reduces trust.

**Action required:** Link to credible sources (red carpet coverage, press photos, entertainment news) for any specific event claims.
